SYDNEY-BASED ARTIST

Phillip Patterson

I make objects that remember what it feels like to break and keep going.

ARTIST STATEMENT

The work is the evidence.

My practice is rooted in the physical act of making and the emotional residue it leaves behind. I am drawn to materials that carry their own history of stress — glass that shatters, pigment that cracks under its own weight, resin that traps air like held breath.

I do not seek to represent emotion. I engineer situations in which emotion can occur. The fractured surface, the trapped bubble, the impossible angle of suspended glass — these are not metaphors. They are the thing itself.

Texture is not decoration. It is the record of force. Light is not illumination. It is the revealer of what was hidden in the making. Frenzy is the necessary condition for anything honest to emerge.

"The absence of my sanity was born somewhere. I have spent years trying to locate the coordinates."

THE STUDIO

Process & Materiality

Every piece begins with a question the material must answer.

Material Experiments

Hydrophilic fumed silica is introduced into wet acrylic to induce controlled micro-fracturing. The timing of the reaction, the humidity of the studio, the thickness of the pour — all become compositional decisions.

Light as Material

Glass and resin are chosen for their refractive properties. Internal structures are built so that light does not simply illuminate the surface but travels through the work, revealing layers that only appear at certain times of day or from specific angles.

Surrender & Control

There is a precise moment in every work where I must stop intervening. The materials continue their transformation after my hands leave. This tension between intention and accident is where the most honest surfaces emerge.

CREATIVE PHILOSOPHY

I am not interested in beauty that has not survived something.

The works exist in a state of permanent becoming. They are never finished in the traditional sense — they continue to respond to their environment, to light, to the passage of time. A crack that was barely visible on day one may become the dominant feature after two years on a wall.

This is not fragility as aesthetic. It is an honest acknowledgment that everything we build — emotional structures included — carries the memory of its own potential collapse. The beauty is in the refusal to hide that truth.

THE SPACE

Inner City Sydney Studio

Working from a compact government housing apartment in Surry Hills, I have transformed limitation into laboratory. The same forces that shape the work — density, pressure, the negotiation of private and public space — are present in the making environment itself.
